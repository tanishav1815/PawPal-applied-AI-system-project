# Pet Nutrition Guidelines

## Dogs — Feeding Frequency

Puppies under 3 months: 4 meals per day.
Puppies 3–6 months: 3 meals per day.
Puppies 6–12 months: 2 meals per day.
Adult dogs (1–7 years): 2 meals per day — morning and evening.
Senior dogs (7+): 2 meals per day, or 3 smaller meals if the dog has digestive issues.

## Cats — Feeding Frequency

Kittens under 6 months: 3–4 small meals per day.
Adult cats (1–7 years): 2 meals per day — morning and evening.
Senior cats (7+): 2–3 meals per day; smaller portions can help cats with sensitive stomachs.

## Portion Size

Overfeeding is one of the most common health problems in domestic pets. Measure portions using a kitchen scale or calibrated measuring cup rather than eyeballing. Always follow the feeding guidelines on the food packaging as a starting point, then adjust based on your pet's body condition score. You should be able to feel but not see your pet's ribs.

## Fresh Water

Both dogs and cats need access to fresh water at all times. Change water daily. Some cats prefer running water and drink more from a pet fountain, which can help prevent urinary tract issues.

## Treats

Treats should make up no more than 10% of a pet's daily calorie intake. High-value treats used in training should be tiny (pea-sized). Avoid giving table scraps — many common human foods are toxic to pets, including grapes, raisins, onions, garlic, chocolate, xylitol, and macadamia nuts.

## Foods to Avoid

The following foods are toxic to dogs and/or cats:
- Chocolate (theobromine poisoning in dogs)
- Grapes and raisins (kidney failure in dogs)
- Onions and garlic (red blood cell damage in both species)
- Xylitol (artificial sweetener; hypoglycemia and liver failure in dogs)
- Alcohol and caffeine
- Raw yeast dough
- Macadamia nuts (dogs)
- Lilies (highly toxic to cats, causing acute kidney failure)

## Reading Food Labels

The first ingredient listed is the most abundant. Look for a named protein source (e.g. "chicken," "salmon") as the first ingredient rather than a grain or by-product. AAFCO-certified foods are formulated to meet minimum nutritional requirements.
