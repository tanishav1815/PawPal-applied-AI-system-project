# Dog Exercise Guidelines

## Daily Exercise Needs by Size

Small dogs (under 10 kg) such as Chihuahuas, Pugs, and Shih Tzus need 20–30 minutes of daily exercise. Two short walks of 10–15 minutes each are usually sufficient. Over-exercising small dogs can stress their joints.

Medium dogs (10–25 kg) such as Border Collies, Cocker Spaniels, and Bulldogs need 30–60 minutes of daily exercise. A mix of one longer walk and one shorter walk works well for most medium breeds.

Large dogs (over 25 kg) such as Labradors, German Shepherds, and Golden Retrievers need 60–90 minutes of daily exercise. High-energy large breeds may need up to 2 hours of activity per day to prevent boredom and destructive behavior.

## Exercise Needs by Age

Puppies (under 12 months) should follow the "5-minute rule": 5 minutes of exercise per month of age, twice daily. For example, a 3-month-old puppy should get no more than 15 minutes per session. Over-exercising puppies can damage developing joints.

Adult dogs (1–7 years) need 30–60 minutes of daily exercise to stay healthy and calm. A 3-year-old dog is in its prime and needs enough exercise every day — at least one 30-minute walk plus playtime. Getting enough daily exercise prevents destructive behavior and keeps adult dogs mentally satisfied. High-energy breeds like Border Collies and Huskies need significantly more exercise than the minimum.

Senior dogs (7+ years) still need regular exercise to maintain muscle tone and joint health, but intensity should be reduced. Shorter, more frequent walks are better than one long session. Watch for signs of fatigue such as lagging behind, heavy panting, or reluctance to continue.

## Types of Exercise

Walking is the most accessible form of exercise and provides mental stimulation through sniffing. Running, fetch, swimming, and agility are excellent for high-energy breeds. Avoid strenuous exercise in hot weather — early morning or evening walks are safer in summer.

## Signs of Insufficient Exercise

Dogs that are under-exercised may display destructive behaviors such as chewing furniture, excessive barking, hyperactivity indoors, or pulling hard on the leash during walks. Increasing daily exercise duration often resolves these issues.
