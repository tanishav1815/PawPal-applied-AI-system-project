# Pet Medication Guidelines

## Importance of Consistent Medication Timing

For most prescribed medications, consistency in timing is critical. Antibiotics, anti-seizure medications, heart medications, and thyroid medications must be given at the same time each day to maintain stable blood levels. Missed doses or irregular timing can reduce effectiveness or cause dangerous fluctuations.

A practical strategy is to tie medication times to your own daily routines — such as giving a pill when you prepare your own morning coffee or evening meal. Using a phone alarm or a weekly pill organizer can prevent missed doses.

## Common Medication Types and Scheduling

Antibiotics: Typically given once or twice daily for 7–14 days. Complete the full course even if your pet seems better; stopping early can cause antibiotic resistance and relapse.

Anti-parasitic medications: Flea/tick/heartworm preventatives are given monthly. Set a recurring reminder on the same date each month. Heartworm prevention must not lapse — a single missed month can allow larvae to mature.

Pain medications (NSAIDs): Should be given with food to reduce stomach irritation. Never give human NSAIDs such as ibuprofen or aspirin to pets — these are toxic to dogs and cats.

Thyroid medication (for cats with hyperthyroidism): Given twice daily, 12 hours apart. Consistent spacing is important for stable hormone control.

Eye/ear drops: Typically given 2–3 times per day. Refrigerate as directed. Warm ear drops to room temperature before use to reduce discomfort.

## Administering Pills

To give a dog a pill, place the pill inside a soft treat, pill pocket, or a small amount of peanut butter. Alternatively, place the pill at the back of the tongue and hold the mouth closed while gently stroking the throat to encourage swallowing.

To give a cat a pill, hold the cat firmly, tilt the head back, open the jaw, and place the pill as far back on the tongue as possible. Close the mouth and gently blow on the nose to trigger swallowing. Pill guns or pill pockets can also make the process easier.

## Guardrails and Safety

Never adjust medication doses without consulting a vet. Do not give two doses if one is missed — skip the missed dose and give the next one at the regular time unless your vet specifies otherwise. Store all medications out of reach of pets and children. Dispose of unused medications safely — do not flush or throw in household trash without checking local guidelines.

If your pet shows vomiting, lethargy, loss of appetite, or unusual behavior after starting a new medication, contact your veterinarian promptly.
