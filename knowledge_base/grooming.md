# Pet Grooming Guidelines

## Dog Grooming by Coat Type

Short-coated dogs (Beagles, Dalmatians, Boxers): Brush once per week with a rubber grooming mitt or bristle brush to remove loose hair. Bathe every 4–6 weeks or when dirty.

Medium-coated dogs (Labradors, Golden Retrievers, Border Collies): Brush 2–3 times per week to remove loose fur and reduce shedding. During heavy shedding seasons (spring and autumn) daily brushing may be needed. Bathe every 4–6 weeks.

Long-coated dogs (Shih Tzus, Maltese, Afghan Hounds): Brush daily to prevent mats and tangles. Professional grooming every 6–8 weeks is recommended for most long-coated breeds.

Double-coated dogs (Huskies, German Shepherds, Chow Chows): Brush at least 3 times per week with an undercoat rake during shedding season. Never shave a double-coated dog — the coat regulates body temperature.

Curly or wavy-coated dogs (Poodles, Labradoodles, Bichon Frises): Brush daily to prevent matting. Professional grooming every 6–8 weeks is essential to trim the coat and prevent dense mats.

## Cat Grooming

Short-haired cats: Brush once or twice per week to reduce shedding and prevent hairballs.

Long-haired cats (Persians, Maine Coons, Ragdolls): Brush daily to prevent painful mats and tangles. Professional grooming may be needed every 6–8 weeks.

## Nail Trimming

Dog nails should be trimmed every 3–4 weeks. Nails that are too long can cause discomfort when walking and may curl into the paw pad. Use a guillotine-style or scissor-style nail clipper and avoid cutting the "quick" (the pink blood vessel inside the nail).

Cat nails should be trimmed every 2–3 weeks. Regular trimming reduces the risk of nails catching on fabric or furniture. Introduce nail trimming gradually for anxious cats.

## Ear Cleaning

Dogs with floppy ears (Spaniels, Basset Hounds) are prone to ear infections and should have their ears checked and cleaned every 1–2 weeks. Use a veterinarian-approved ear cleaner. Signs of infection include odor, discharge, or frequent head shaking.

## Teeth Brushing

Dental disease affects the majority of dogs and cats over age 3. Brushing your pet's teeth 3–5 times per week with pet-safe toothpaste significantly reduces plaque buildup. Dental chews and water additives can supplement brushing but are not a substitute. Professional dental cleanings under anesthesia are recommended by most vets annually or every two years.
